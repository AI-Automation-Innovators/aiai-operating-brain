# Workflow Leak Audit Framework

## Purpose

Provide a practical operating structure for the Workflow Leak Audit.

The audit should help identify where leads, customers, estimates, appointments, follow-ups, ownership, timing, or information get delayed, dropped, or hidden inside an existing business workflow.

## Audit lens

Look for points where attention, ownership, timing, or information breaks down between a customer action and the business response.

The audit starts with the workflow, not the tool.

## Intake questions

### Business context

- What does the business sell or provide?
- Who usually contacts the business first?
- What counts as a good lead, inquiry, appointment, estimate, or customer opportunity?
- Which workflows matter most right now?
- What does the owner suspect is leaking, slowing down, or getting missed?

### Lead and inquiry capture

- Where do new inquiries arrive?
- Which sources are most important?
- Are all inquiry sources visible in one place?
- Who owns first response?
- How quickly does first response usually happen?
- What happens if the first owner is busy, off, or unavailable?

### Follow-up and pipeline movement

- What happens after the first reply?
- Are quotes, estimates, or proposals tracked?
- Are there reminders for second and third touches?
- Does follow-up stop when the owner or staff gets busy?
- How are stale quotes, open estimates, no-shows, cancellations, and inactive customers reviewed?

### Handoffs and ownership

- Where does responsibility move between people or tools?
- Who owns each next step?
- What information gets retyped, missed, or misunderstood?
- Are handoffs visible to the owner or manager?
- Where does work depend on memory instead of a visible system?

### Customer recovery and retention

- Are no-shows, cancellations, stale quotes, or inactive customers reviewed?
- Is there a process for reactivation or follow-up recovery?
- Are completed jobs used for reviews, referrals, repeat work, or next-step offers?
- Are customer recovery tasks assigned and tracked?

### Visibility and reporting

- Can the owner quickly see what is stuck?
- Can the team see who owns each next step?
- Are important workflows hidden in email, text, spreadsheets, calendars, CRMs, or personal memory?
- Which reports, lists, dashboards, or status views are missing?
- What would the owner want to know at a glance each week?

## Leak categories

### 1. Lead capture leaks

A lead capture leak happens when a new inquiry enters the business but is not captured, assigned, responded to, or made visible quickly enough.

Common signs:

- Inquiry sources are spread across inboxes, calls, forms, texts, DMs, spreadsheets, calendars, or CRMs.
- First response ownership is unclear.
- Staff rely on memory to notice or assign new inquiries.
- No simple view exists for new, unanswered, or aging inquiries.

### 2. Follow-up leaks

A follow-up leak happens when an opportunity receives an initial response but does not continue moving through a clear next-step process.

Common signs:

- Quotes, estimates, or proposals are sent once and forgotten.
- Follow-up depends on whoever remembers.
- No clear second-touch or third-touch process exists.
- Stale opportunities are not reviewed.

### 3. Handoff leaks

A handoff leak happens when responsibility moves between people, stages, or tools without clear ownership, timing, or information transfer.

Common signs:

- Staff are unsure who owns the next step.
- Notes are copied between tools by hand.
- Details get lost between intake, scheduling, estimating, delivery, billing, or follow-up.
- The owner becomes the manual router for too many decisions.

### 4. Customer recovery leaks

A customer recovery leak happens when a business has a reason to re-engage a customer or prospect but no consistent process for doing so.

Common signs:

- No-shows, cancellations, stale quotes, and inactive customers are not reviewed.
- Completed jobs do not consistently trigger review requests, referrals, repeat work, or next-step offers.
- Recovery opportunities live in disconnected tools or staff memory.

### 5. Visibility leaks

A visibility leak happens when important work exists somewhere in the business but cannot be seen, prioritized, or managed easily.

Common signs:

- The owner cannot quickly see what is stuck.
- Work status is hidden in email, texts, spreadsheets, calendars, CRMs, or personal memory.
- No shared view exists for open opportunities, pending follow-ups, aging estimates, or owner decisions.

## Scoring criteria

Score each finding from `1` to `5` for each criterion.

- `1` = low concern or limited evidence.
- `3` = meaningful concern worth addressing.
- `5` = high concern with clear operational impact.

### Criteria

- Revenue or opportunity impact.
- Frequency.
- Ease of fixing.
- Owner or staff pain.
- Customer experience impact.
- Visibility and accountability gap.

### Priority score

Add the criteria scores for a simple priority score.

Use the score to rank findings, not to imply guaranteed revenue recovery.

## Report sections

A practical Workflow Leak Audit report should include:

1. Executive summary.
2. Current workflow snapshot.
3. Confirmed tools and touchpoints reviewed.
4. Key workflow leaks found.
5. Scored and ranked findings.
6. Recommended practical fixes.
7. Suggested AI operating-layer improvements, if useful.
8. Boundaries, assumptions, and `TBD` items.
9. Suggested next steps.

## Example finding format

```markdown
## Finding: [Short name]

- Workflow area: [Lead capture / follow-up / handoff / customer recovery / visibility]
- Leak observed: [What is delayed, dropped, unclear, duplicated, or hidden]
- Evidence/source: [Interview note, workflow walkthrough, tool view, document, or TBD]
- Business impact: [Plain-English impact without unsupported revenue claims]
- Current owner: [Person/role or TBD]
- Current tools involved: [Inbox, texts, spreadsheet, calendar, CRM, staff memory, or TBD]
- Score: [Total score and brief reason]
- Recommended fix: [Practical next step]
- AI operating-layer opportunity: [If useful; otherwise "Not recommended yet"]
- Boundary: [What is not being promised or included]
```

## Recommendation style

Recommendations should be:

- Practical.
- Specific.
- Workflow-first.
- Tool-aware.
- Clear about ownership and next steps.
- Honest about unknowns.

Recommendations should not require a new system unless the workflow clearly needs one.

## Boundaries

- This is an audit, not a full automation build unless separately scoped.
- Do not promise guaranteed recovered revenue.
- Do not invent benchmark data, client proof, testimonials, or results.
- Do not assume a tool migration is needed.
- Do not assume implementation is included.
- Mark unknowns as `TBD`.
